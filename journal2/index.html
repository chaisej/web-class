Little Constellation of Technology

The Web’s Grain by Frank Chimero

Frank Chimero’s “The Web’s Grain” is a reflection on the evolution of web design. Chimero begins by invoking nostalgia for the sounds of dial-up internet connection—which I have never heard—, to highlight the transformative experience of the early internet.

He centers on the "edgelessness" power of web design, showcasing David Hockney’s work to illustrate his point. Chimero argues that the traditional approach of designing within fixed boundaries, on a canvas, is outdated when the web is infinite. Instead, he advocates for an approach where elements are assembled without constraints, championing fluid and responsive design. Chimero talks about the challenges of responsive design, paying close attention to the tension between images and text scaling differently.

He illuminates the impact of technology, expressing concerns about an over-reliance on convenience and the loss of autonomy. Chimero advocates for a mindful approach to technology—and suggests drawing and creating first before adding a constricting web frame.

Chimero envisions a “technology of grace” that aligns with intentions, promoting mindfulness and intentional consideration of technology's role in our lives. He desires a technology that integrates with the human experience, removing unnecessary complexity and aligning with individuality.

I agree with Chimero’s argument about the danger of confining or defining technology. I love that he writes about the “little constellation of technology” that exists today. We never move on from a technology, it simply becomes integrated into our cultural cloud. Dial phones have become touchscreen; cars have become gasless and run by computers. Technology is never left behind. Using “constellation” as a metaphor struck me—technology is a web of connections but also there is an awe we experience in its presence like looking up at Orion or the Big Dipper. Technology must be limitless, and we must adapt to reimagine an unbounded canvas.
